Module 1 — Foundations

What GEO is, what LLMO is, and what Semrush is really helping you do.

The central shift

Traditional SEO is organized around the question:

Do we rank in search results?

GEO reframes the question into:

Do AI systems mention, recommend, or cite us when users ask relevant questions?

That shift matters because AI answers can compress research, comparison, and evaluation into a single response. Instead of just winning a blue link, a brand now needs to win space in the answer itself or in the evidence stack behind that answer.

Classic SEO focus GEO / AIO focus Practical implication
Where do we rank? Do we appear in AI answers? You need answer-level visibility, not only SERP-level visibility.
What keywords matter? What prompts matter? You need richer intent modeling than short query phrases.
Can we earn the click? Can we become the cited or recommended answer? You need content that is useful for synthesis and recommendation, not just ranking.

Key terms in plain English

GEO Generative Engine Optimization — optimizing so your brand and content can appear inside AI-generated answers.

LLMO Large Language Model Optimization — similar family of work, but framed more directly around LLM systems and their behavior.

AI Visibility — the measurable outcome: are you present, on which prompts, in what position, and with what citations or mention patterns?

Prompt — the actual question or request a user gives an AI system, often with constraints and context.

Citation — when an AI system references a specific page as supporting evidence or source material.

ICP — Ideal Customer Profile, meaning the type of customer you most want and are best suited to win.

ICP example

If your product is enterprise HR software, the prompt best free hr tool for freelancers may be a real prompt, but it is likely low ICP fit. A better ICP-fit prompt would be something like what HR system is best for a multi-country mid-market company.

Prompts vs keywords

A keyword is usually a compressed phrase. A prompt is often a fuller expression of need, situation, and evaluation logic. This is one of the most important conceptual shifts in GEO.

Keyword style Prompt style What the prompt adds
best crm software "What CRM is best for a 200-person SaaS sales team?" Context, likely budget, team size, real buying scenario
asana alternative "What are the best Asana alternatives for agency client work?" Buyer type, use case, job-to-be-done
payroll software "Which payroll tool is easiest for remote-first startups?" User segment plus evaluation criterion
Memory hook: prompts are usually closer to the actual decision moment than bare keywords.

Mentions vs recommendations vs citations

Level What it means Why it matters
Mention Your brand is named somewhere in the answer. Basic presence, but often shallow.
Recommendation Your brand is presented as an option or shortlist choice. Stronger commercial value.
Citation Your page is used as evidence or support. Often the strongest practical sign of trust and content usefulness.

Example

If an AI answer says "Flowdesk, Mailchimp, and Klaviyo are common email platforms," that is a mention. If it says "Flowdesk is often preferred by smaller ecommerce teams for simplicity," that is closer to a recommendation. If it links to your lifecycle automation guide as support, that is a citation.

Where Semrush fits

Layer Role Examples
AI answer systems Generate or synthesize answers ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, AI Mode
Your site and content The pages that may be cited or summarized product pages, FAQs, comparisons, guides
Semrush Measures and guides visibility strategy Prompt Research, Prompt Tracking, Cited Pages, AI Search audit
Best one-line definition: Semrush helps you understand where you stand in AI discovery and what that suggests you should do next.

What to remember from Module 1

  • GEO shifts the question from ranking to recommendation and citation.
  • Prompts are richer than keywords and often map more directly to buyer behavior.
  • Mentions are useful; citations are usually more valuable.
  • Semrush is not the LLM; it is the measurement and intelligence layer around AI discovery.

Module 2 — AI Visibility Toolkit

How to understand the product as a coordinated system, not disconnected screens.

The AI Visibility Toolkit is easiest to understand as a system built around one master question:

How visible are we in AI answers, why, and what should we do next?
Feature area Main job Best question it answers
Visibility Overview Top-level AI presence monitoring How visible are we overall?
Prompt Research Discover important prompts and prompt clusters What should we target?
Prompt Tracking Monitor the selected operating set over time Are we improving on priority prompts?
Cited Pages Show which URLs AI systems actually use Which pages does AI trust enough to cite?
Competitor views Gap and comparison analysis Who is beating us, and where?
Site Audit → AI Search Technical AI-readiness checks Are technical issues suppressing visibility?

Visibility Overview

This is the pulse check. It is useful for seeing whether your brand is:

  • broadly present,
  • weak overall,
  • visible only in a few topic clusters,
  • or losing valuable space to competitors.

The mistake is to stop at the top-level signal. The better move is to use it as an entry point into prompts, citations, and competitor patterns.

Prompt Research

This is where Semrush becomes strategically useful. It helps build the prompt universe around your category, brand, and buyer scenarios.

Prompt Tracking

Once you know what matters, this becomes your operational measurement layer. It is the closest analogue to rank tracking, but for AI surfaces and prompt portfolios.

Cited Pages

Often the most actionable view for content teams. It tells you which URLs are already doing the work of earning AI trust.

How to teach the Toolkit well

A common mistake is to teach the Toolkit as six dashboards. The stronger teaching sequence is:

  1. Find the prompt landscape
  2. Track the prompts that matter
  3. See which pages win citations
  4. See which competitors own the gaps
  5. Check whether technical blockers explain the misses
Teaching note: a useful phrase here is: "The Toolkit is a GEO decision system, not just a monitoring interface."
Important nuance: Semrush does not usually hand customers a fully finished GEO roadmap. It surfaces the evidence that allows a team to create one.

What to remember from Module 2

  • Visibility Overview tells you where you stand.
  • Prompt Research tells you what to care about.
  • Prompt Tracking tells you whether you are moving.
  • Cited Pages and competitor analysis tell you where the roadmap likely is.
  • AI Search audit tells you whether technical friction is suppressing results.

Module 3 — Prompt Research

How to build prompt sets that are strategic, commercially useful, and operationally manageable.

What Prompt Research is really for

Prompt Research is not just "find a bunch of AI questions." It is how you identify the prompts that best represent:

  • buyer intent,
  • category demand,
  • AI recommendation opportunities, and
  • competitive pressure.
Better definition: Prompt Research builds a usable prompt universe, not a random list.

How seed expansion works in practice

Start with a seed topic like crm software. The goal is not to create lexical variants of the same idea. The goal is to expand across intent, scenario, and buying context.

Expansion type Example Why it matters
Category "What CRM is best for small businesses?" Defines the broad market conversation.
Alternative "What are the best HubSpot alternatives?" Captures switching and replacement behavior.
Comparison "HubSpot vs Salesforce for B2B teams" Strong buyer evaluation signal.
Use case "What CRM is best for outbound sales teams?" Maps product to job-to-be-done.
Evaluation "Which CRM is easiest to implement?" Surfaces differentiators and decision criteria.
Simple rule: seed expansion = topic expansion + intent expansion + buying-context expansion.

The four prompt buckets you should build first

1. Category prompts

These tell you whether you are even in the core category conversation.

Example: "What is the best endpoint security platform?"

2. Comparison / alternatives

Often among the highest-intent prompts in GEO work.

Example: "What are good Klaviyo alternatives for smaller teams?"

3. Use-case prompts

These connect the product to a practical scenario.

Example: "Which payroll tool is best for remote startups?"

4. Evaluation prompts

These surface selection criteria like price, ease, implementation, reporting, or integrations.

Example: "Which CRM is easiest to implement?"

Research set vs tracking set

Set type Purpose Best shape
Research set Explore the full prompt universe Broad, exploratory, intentionally expansive
Tracking set Run the GEO operating program Narrow, prioritized, commercially useful
Rule: research wide, track narrow.

How to prioritize prompts properly

A useful prompt is not just one that looks interesting. A useful prompt is one you can justify operationally.

  • Would winning this prompt matter to the business?
  • Is the buyer near a decision?
  • Should our brand plausibly appear here?
  • Do we have a candidate page or credible path to one?
  • Is a competitor already winning this space?

How real are these prompts?

This is where precision matters. The safest explanation is:

Semrush prompt discovery is best treated as market-grounded directional intelligence, not a perfect transcript of private user LLM chats.

The right workflow is:

  1. Use Semrush to generate candidate prompt demand.
  2. Filter against ICP and commercial relevance.
  3. Validate against first-party signals such as site search, sales calls, and customer language.
  4. Move the strongest prompts into the tracking set.

Worked example: B2B cybersecurity

Seed: endpoint security

Likely prompt cluster expansion:

  • "What is the best endpoint security platform for enterprises?"
  • "CrowdStrike alternatives for mid-market businesses"
  • "Which endpoint security tool is easiest to deploy?"
  • "Best endpoint security for remote workforces"
  • "SentinelOne vs CrowdStrike for managed detection"

This already suggests the likely page mix: comparison pages, deployment FAQ or guide pages, role- or scenario-based pages, and a solid category explainer.

What to remember from Module 3

  • Prompt Research builds a prompt universe, not just a list.
  • Seed expansion should create prompt families, not just phrasing variants.
  • Research sets are broad; tracking sets are curated.
  • Semrush gives candidate demand; first-party data gives customer truth.

Module 4 — Prompt Tracking

How to interpret movement, avoid false confidence, and use tracking to guide action.

What Prompt Tracking is really for

A weak mental model is:

"Did we show up?"

A stronger mental model is:

"Across the prompts we care about, are we becoming more visible, more prominent, or more cite-worthy over time?"
Metric Plain English meaning Best use
AI Visibility Overall presence across the tracked set Portfolio-level trend
Mentions Whether the brand appears at all Presence check, but incomplete on its own
Average Position How prominently you show up Strength and prominence interpretation
Trends over time Direction of movement over weeks Signal vs noise separation

False positives

  • One prompt improved, so the program is working.
  • Mentions went up, so success.
  • A single strong week means strategic improvement.
  • Only branded prompts improved, so category visibility is healthy.

Better reading habits

  • Track a portfolio, not vanity prompts.
  • Read movement by cluster.
  • Look at multi-week patterns.
  • Connect changes to known content or technical actions.

How to read tracked prompts by cluster

Cluster If it improves Likely interpretation
Category You appear more in broad category prompts Brand/category relevance is improving
Comparison / alternatives You show up more in shortlist prompts Strong sign of GEO progress
Use case You are appearing more in scenario-based prompts Content-market fit may be improving
Evaluation You win more on prompts about implementation, price, fit, ease, or integrations Decision-stage trust is improving

Example

A payroll software company tracks 20 prompts. After publishing two comparison pages and one remote-team FAQ:

  • comparison prompts improve clearly
  • remote-team use-case prompts improve slightly
  • enterprise prompts stay flat

Good conclusion: the new content is working in startup / remote clusters, but the company still lacks strong enterprise-specific coverage.

What to do with Prompt Tracking data

If you see... Then the next likely move is...
Comparison prompts improving Double down on alternatives and comparison assets
Use-case prompts improving Expand role-, scenario-, or industry-specific pages
Category prompts flat Strengthen core category pages and supporting explainers
Mentions up but citations flat Improve clarity, evidence, structure, and page usefulness
Strong volatility with no clear pattern Wait for more trend data before overreacting
Teaching note: if you want someone to "get" Prompt Tracking quickly, teach them to stop looking prompt-by-prompt first and start looking cluster-by-cluster.

Review cadence

Cadence Purpose
Weekly Spot movement and anomalies
Monthly Interpret real trends and decide strategy changes
Quarterly Refresh prompt set and reset operating priorities
Best operator rule: tracking should always change what you do next. If it does not, you are collecting charts, not running GEO.

Module 5 — Cited Pages and competitor analysis

Where Semrush becomes most useful for roadmap thinking.

Why Cited Pages matters so much

Cited Pages is one of the strongest practical "trust" signals in Semrush. It tells you which of your URLs AI systems actually use as support or evidence.

Better question: not "which page should matter?" but "which page already matters to AI?"

Common citation winners often include:

comparison pages FAQ pages glossaries use-case pages buyer guides stats pages

Example

A cybersecurity company expects its main product page to be the citation center. Instead, the highest-cited pages are:

  • zero trust explainer
  • endpoint protection buyer guide
  • vendor comparison page

The correct takeaway is not "rewrite the homepage." It is "AI trusts our educational and comparison content more than our commercial overview pages."

How to interpret a cited page

  • What type of page is it?
  • Which prompt family likely triggered it?
  • Why might AI prefer it?
  • Is this a strategically good page to have cited?
  • Can we build a broader content cluster around it?

Competitor analysis: what it is really for

Competitor analysis is not just "who appears more." Its real value is diagnosing where competitors are winning, on which prompts, with which page types, and what that implies about your gaps.

Question Why it matters
Which competitors appear where we are absent? Identifies clear visibility gaps.
What prompt types do they win? Shows whether they dominate category, comparison, use-case, or evaluation moments.
What page types seem to support those wins? Suggests what content format AI may prefer in that topic area.
Are they winning by breadth or by depth? Changes how you respond strategically.

Does Semrush generate roadmap work items automatically?

Mostly no. Semrush surfaces the evidence, but the team still translates that evidence into work items.

Semrush can show:

  • prompts you miss,
  • competitors that win them,
  • pages that are cited,
  • patterns in winning content.

The team still decides:

  • create vs rewrite vs reformat,
  • which content cluster gets priority,
  • who owns the action,
  • whether the gap is ICP-relevant or only visibly interesting.

How to convert Module 5 into a roadmap

Observed pattern Likely response
Competitor wins alternatives prompts Create or strengthen alternatives pages
Your glossary pages get cited, but evaluation pages do not Add buyer guides, pricing explainers, implementation FAQs
Important page exists but is never cited Improve structure, proof, specificity, or format
No asset exists for a high-intent cluster Create the missing asset
Best mental model: Cited Pages tells you what AI already trusts. Competitor analysis tells you where trust or presence is missing. The roadmap is the set of pages you need to defend, improve, or create.

What to remember from Module 5

  • Do not assume the homepage should be the main citation target.
  • Cited Pages often reveals the real center of gravity of AI trust.
  • Competitor analysis matters only if it ends in page hypotheses and actions.
  • Semrush informs the roadmap, but humans still create the roadmap.

Module 6 — Site Audit for AI Search

The technical layer of GEO, without the hype.

Good GEO content can still underperform if AI systems cannot easily access, parse, or retrieve it. This is why technical readiness matters.

The four technical buckets that matter most

Bucket Core question
Access Can AI crawlers reach the content?
Discovery Can they find the important pages easily?
Structure Is the content organized in a machine-friendly way?
Clarity Is the meaning explicit enough to use?

What matters most

  • Access — can AI bots reach the page?
  • Discovery — can they find the page easily?
  • Structure — is the content organized clearly?
  • Clarity — is the meaning explicit enough to use?

What people over-focus on

  • llms.txt as a silver bullet
  • novelty checks over core crawlability
  • homepage readiness over high-value supporting pages
If AI crawlers cannot access important pages, content work gets undercut before it starts.

What AI-ready content usually looks like

  • clear headings and section logic,
  • explicit answers and definitions,
  • useful internal links,
  • supporting structure such as lists and tables where helpful,
  • less ambiguity and less marketing fog.

Example

Weak: "We revolutionize adaptive orchestration across dynamic workflow surfaces."

Better: "Our platform automates invoice approvals across ERP systems and reduces manual review steps."

Where llms.txt fits

Good framing: llms.txt is a supporting AI-readiness signal.
Bad framing: llms.txt is the core GEO strategy.

If a site still has blocked pages, weak internal links, confusing structure, or low-clarity content, adding llms.txt will not rescue the program.

How to prioritize technical fixes

Priority Technical issue type
High Important page blocked or inaccessible to AI agents
High High-value page buried, orphaned, or poorly linked
Medium Weak structure, ambiguous content layout, missing helpful schema
Low Hygiene issues on low-value or non-supporting pages
Teaching note: the right tone here is calm. Technical GEO is usually not about chasing every shiny AI-specific artifact. It is mostly about making valuable content reachable, discoverable, and understandable.

What to remember from Module 6

  • Technical GEO is mostly classic accessibility, crawlability, structure, and clarity reframed for AI retrieval.
  • Access comes first.
  • Internal linking and page structure matter more than many teams realize.
  • llms.txt is supportive, not central.

Module 7 — Practical weekly GEO workflow

How to operate Semrush without drowning in dashboards.
Operating loop: Discover → Measure → Diagnose → Fix → Review
Cadence What to do Why it matters
Weekly Review prompt movement, citation changes, competitor shifts, and technical blockers Stay current without overreacting
Monthly Interpret cluster performance and roadmap impact Separate signal from noise
Quarterly Refresh prompt universe and operating set Keep the program aligned to current demand

Weekly GEO review format

  1. What improved?
  2. What worsened?
  3. Which competitor moved?
  4. Which page or prompt cluster matters most next?
  5. What 1–2 actions will we take this week?
Rule of thumb: one or two meaningful actions per cycle beats ten scattered tweaks.

A realistic weekly operating rhythm

Monday — Review

Check Prompt Tracking, Cited Pages, competitor gaps, and AI Search issues.

Tuesday — Diagnose

Decide whether the problem is missing content, weak content, wrong page type, or technical friction.

Wednesday — Decide

Pick the next intervention: create, rewrite, reformat, or fix.

Thursday / Friday — Execute and log

Implement the change and record it so future movement is interpretable.

Example

A CRM brand sees weak visibility for "HubSpot alternatives," a strong glossary citation pattern, and poor internal links to comparison pages.

Correct next step: strengthen internal links and rewrite one alternatives page. If comparison prompts then improve and that page begins getting cited, the next step is to expand adjacent comparison coverage.

How to make review cycles useful

Every review cycle should end with:

  • what changed,
  • why it matters,
  • what we think caused it,
  • what we will do next.

What to remember from Module 7

  • Weekly review is for movement.
  • Monthly review is for interpretation.
  • Quarterly review is for resetting the prompt system.
  • Semrush becomes powerful when it feeds a cadence, not random exploration.

Module 8 — Common mistakes and how to avoid them

The main failure modes in Semrush GEO work.

The biggest mistakes

  • confusing prompt research with literal prompt truth
  • tracking everything you research
  • watching mentions while ignoring citations
  • assuming the homepage should be the citation winner
  • turning every gap into "write a blog post"
  • overvaluing llms.txt
  • ignoring technical blockers
  • spending more time monitoring than optimizing

The fix pattern

  • validate with first-party reality
  • track a curated prompt portfolio
  • prioritize cited pages and prompt clusters
  • choose the right page type
  • fix access before polishing copy
  • turn every review cycle into action

Common misconception table

If someone says... What it usually means Better correction
"We track lots of prompts." The program may lack prioritization. Track fewer, better prompts.
"Our mentions are up." Presence may be improving, but trust may not be. Check citations and prompt quality, not just mention count.
"Competitor X is winning." The diagnosis is incomplete. Identify which prompts and which page types explain the gap.
"We added llms.txt." AI hygiene may be mistaken for strategy. Check access, internal links, structure, and clarity first.
"We spend a lot of time in Semrush." The team may be watching more than acting. End every cycle with concrete work items.
One-line summary: most Semrush GEO mistakes happen when teams confuse visibility intelligence with completed optimization.

What to remember from Module 8

  • Semrush is strongest when it helps teams decide where to act.
  • Operational discipline matters more than dashboard volume.
  • Citations, page types, and competitor gaps usually matter more than vanity signal reading.

Appendix — Semrush vs Adobe LLM Optimizer

The most honest explanation is not "there is no overlap." The overlap is real. The better explanation is that the two products have different centers of gravity.

Best clean distinction:
Semrush = intelligence and discovery
Adobe LLM Optimizer = activation and optimization
Question Semrush LLMO
What prompts matter? Strongest for broad prompt universe discovery Better as the curated operating set
What is happening in the market? Core strength Secondary
What is happening on our owned properties? Limited compared with Adobe Core strength
What should we change next? Signals and inferred priorities Stronger prescriptive / activation story
Can we tie this to business outcomes? Weaker Stronger

Best explanation of prompt overlap

In Semrush, prompts are mainly a discovery and benchmarking instrument.
In LLMO, prompts are mainly an operating control set.

That distinction matters because otherwise both products can sound like "prompt tracking" and "AI monitoring," which makes LLMO prompt pricing harder to defend when Semrush is already in the picture.

Best short talk tracks

  • Executive: "Semrush shows you the AI market; Adobe LLM Optimizer helps you win on your properties and prove impact."
  • Practitioner: "Semrush finds the GEO opportunities; LLMO helps you operationalize them."
  • Sales / readiness: "Semrush is the intelligence layer; LLMO is the activation layer."

Glossary

GEO Optimization for visibility in AI-generated answers.

AIO In this context, AI visibility / AI optimization framing used around discovery and monitoring.

LLMO Optimization framed around large language models and their answer behavior.

Prompt A real-world user question to an AI system.

High intent A prompt closer to action, selection, switching, or purchase.

Citation A URL used by an AI system as support or evidence.

Cited Page A page from your site referenced in AI answers.

ICP Ideal Customer Profile.

Prompt universe The broad set of prompts relevant to your category and brand.

Tracking set The curated set of prompts you monitor operationally.

Quick operating checklist

Weekly

  • Review Prompt Tracking for movement by cluster.
  • Check Cited Pages for new or lost citation winners.
  • Inspect competitor gains on high-intent prompts.
  • Check AI Search blockers on important pages.
  • Choose 1–2 actions only.

Monthly

  • Interpret portfolio trends, not daily noise.
  • Review whether category, comparison, use-case, and evaluation clusters are balanced.
  • Decide which page clusters need to be created, improved, or expanded.

Quarterly

  • Refresh seed topics and prompt universe.
  • Remove stale prompts.
  • Add emerging prompts.
  • Reassess whether the tracked set still reflects business priorities.
