Building a Strong Prompt Bank for Trial Success — Complete Guide

A prompt bank is one of the most important ingredients in a successful trial. It defines the customer questions you want to test, the business areas you want to learn from, and the visibility opportunities you want to improve. This guide brings together the key ideas, design principles, examples, and operating practices needed to build a prompt bank that is focused, practical, and ready to generate useful insight.

Section 1

What a prompt bank is

A prompt bank is a structured collection of customer-style questions that represents the scenarios, intents, and business priorities you want to monitor during a trial. It helps you move from broad assumptions to a more disciplined view of where your brand is visible, where your content is strong, and where there may be gaps or missed opportunities.

Categories

Business areas

These are the broad parts of the business you want to explore, such as Business Banking, Card Services, Wealth, Loans, or Digital Banking.

Topics

Scenario clusters

These are the question groups inside a category, such as Account Opening, Fees, Rewards, Cross-border Payments, App Access, or Disputes.

Prompts

Individual questions

These are the actual customer-style prompts that will be used to test visibility, identify patterns, and surface opportunities to improve.

Helpful mental model: Think of the prompt bank as a structured question library. Its role is not just to collect prompts — it is to define what the trial is trying to learn.
Section 2

Why the prompt bank matters

The quality of the prompt bank strongly influences the quality of the trial. A well-built bank makes it easier to interpret results, spot meaningful patterns, compare performance across scenarios, and decide what to improve next.

A strong prompt bank helps you
  • focus the trial on the business questions that matter most
  • cover a useful mix of customer intents and decision moments
  • spot which areas are performing well and which need attention
  • avoid wasting prompts on vague, overlapping, or low-value questions
  • create a more stable baseline for observing change over time
A weak prompt bank often leads to
  • generic results that are difficult to interpret
  • too much overlap between topics
  • important customer journeys being missed
  • unclear recommendations about what to improve
  • noise instead of useful insight
Key principle: Better prompts produce better learning. A trial does not need the largest possible prompt bank — it needs one that is focused, representative, and practical.
Section 3

How to structure a strong prompt bank

The best prompt banks are built in three layers: business areas, scenario clusters, and individual prompts. The structure should be simple enough to understand, but detailed enough to reflect real customer needs.

1

Choose the scope

Decide which business area or trial theme matters most right now.

2

Define categories

Use broad business areas that reflect meaningful customer needs.

3

Group into topics

Organize related questions into clear scenario clusters.

4

Write prompts

Create realistic, natural-language questions for each topic.

5

Review overlap

Remove duplicates, overly broad items, and unclear topic boundaries.

6

Finalize for baseline

Keep the first version stable enough to create a meaningful starting point.

Layer Purpose Good practice Common mistake
Category Defines the business area Use a small number of clear, meaningful categories Using internal labels or too many low-priority categories
Topic Groups related customer scenarios Make each topic focused enough to interpret easily Topics that are too broad or too narrow
Prompt Tests an individual customer-style question Use natural phrasing and enough context to be specific Generic, repetitive, or overly leading prompts
Best practice for trials: It is usually better to go deeper on a smaller number of topics than to spread a limited prompt bank too thinly.
Section 4

What good prompts look like

A good prompt feels like a real customer question. It should be clear, specific, and focused enough to produce useful and interpretable results.

Natural

Use language that sounds like a real question, not a keyword list.

Specific

Include enough context to reduce ambiguity.

Focused

Keep each prompt centered on one clear scenario or need.

Actionable

Choose prompts that can lead to useful interpretation and next steps.

Distinct

Avoid prompts that repeat the same intent with only minor wording changes.

Example: broad vs stronger prompt

Too broad

What is the best business bank account?

This is likely to lead to a very generic answer because it does not specify the type of business, use case, or need.

Stronger

What is the best business bank account for startups in Singapore?

This is easier to interpret because it defines the audience and narrows the scenario.

Example: overlapping vs differentiated prompts

Overlapping

How do I waive my annual fee?
How do I request an annual fee waiver?

These are very close in meaning and may not both be needed.

Better differentiated

How do I request an annual fee waiver?
Why was my annual fee waiver request rejected?

These questions are related, but each serves a more distinct purpose.

Prompt quality test Strong prompt Weak prompt
Specificity Defines audience, need, or context Too general to interpret easily
Clarity Easy to understand in one reading Bundles too many questions together
Distinctiveness Adds a new angle or scenario Repeats another prompt with only minor wording changes
Practical value Likely to produce useful insight Too vague to guide a next step
Section 5

How to think about trial strategy

Trial prompt banks should be designed for learning, not for maximum volume. The goal is to choose the right questions, not to cover every possible question on day one.

Coverage

Include the right scenarios

Make sure the prompt bank covers the areas that matter most to the business, such as onboarding, comparison, support, transactions, or product selection.

Balance

Mix different intents

A useful trial usually includes a mix of discovery, comparison, problem-solving, and operational questions.

Stability

Protect the baseline

Keep the core set stable long enough to create a meaningful reference point before changing large parts of the bank.

A practical trial prompt mix may include
  • questions about choosing between options
  • questions about opening, activating, or getting started
  • questions about fees, waivers, or eligibility
  • questions about access, support, or troubleshooting
  • questions tied to high-priority products or services
Common trial mistakes
  • trying to cover too many categories at once
  • creating too many narrow topics with only one prompt each
  • overloading the bank with broad benchmark prompts
  • changing the entire set before a baseline is established
  • treating volume as more important than clarity
Important trial nuance: A more brand-forward prompt mix can be reasonable in a limited pilot if it aligns with the business goal. The key is to make sure that the mix is intentional and that the prompt bank still covers the most important questions clearly.
Section 6

How to build, review, and refine the bank

Building a prompt bank is not a one-time writing exercise. It is an iterative process: select the right scope, draft the first version, refine what is weak, then hold the bank stable long enough to learn from it.

Step 1

Gather inputs

Start from real customer questions, product priorities, service scenarios, and frequent decision points.

Step 2

Draft structure

Create categories, topics, and a first prompt set with enough depth in the most important areas.

Step 3

Review quality

Remove overlaps, narrow broad prompts, and merge topics that are too fragmented.

Step 4

Refine selectively

Change the weakest parts first, not the entire prompt bank at once.

Review question What to look for
Are the categories meaningful? They should represent real business areas, not internal shorthand or arbitrary buckets.
Are the topics usable? They should be broad enough to form a cluster, but not so broad that everything fits inside them.
Are the prompts too broad? Look for generic "best" questions without enough context.
Are prompts overlapping? Remove near-duplicates unless each one adds a clearly different scenario.
Is the bank too fragmented? In smaller trials, too many one-prompt topics often weaken the overall structure.
Can you explain why each prompt is there? If not, it may not be adding enough value.
Review principle: The goal is not to create the most prompts. The goal is to create the most useful set of prompts for the trial you are actually running.

Section 8

Roles and ownership

Prompt banks are strongest when they are built collaboratively. Different teams often contribute different kinds of knowledge.

Marketing

Helps identify priority products, campaigns, and positioning themes.

Product

Adds knowledge about features, use cases, and customer decision drivers.

Service / Support

Contributes real customer problem statements, pain points, and common scenarios.

Digital / Content

Helps translate the insights into clearer information architecture and content improvements.

Ownership model: One team should coordinate the structure, but the strongest prompt banks are usually cross-functional.
Section 9

Frequently asked questions

How many prompts should we start with?

Start with enough prompts to cover your highest-priority topics meaningfully, but not so many that the trial becomes unfocused. In most cases, a compact, well-structured bank is more useful than a large, loosely organized one.

Should every topic have the same number of prompts?

Not necessarily. The prompt count should reflect business priority and scenario depth. What matters most is that important topics have enough coverage to be useful.

Should we include both broad and specific prompts?

Yes, but use broad prompts selectively. A few broad benchmark questions can be helpful, while the rest of the bank should be specific enough to create more interpretable signals.

How often should we change the prompt bank during a trial?

Avoid changing large parts of the bank too quickly. It is usually better to hold the core set stable long enough to establish a baseline, then refine the weakest prompts selectively.

Is it a problem if many prompts mention the brand?

Not necessarily. In a focused trial, a more brand-forward prompt bank can be appropriate if that matches the goal. The important thing is that the mix is intentional and that the prompts still represent the questions you most want to test.

What is the biggest sign that a prompt bank needs work?

Usually it is one of three things: the topics are too fragmented, the prompts are too broad, or too many prompts overlap in meaning.

Section 10

Complete trial readiness checklist

Use this checklist before finalizing a prompt bank for a trial.

Structure checklist
  • Have we chosen a clear and focused category or business scope?
  • Are the topics broad enough to form useful clusters, but narrow enough to stay clear?
  • Have we avoided creating too many one-prompt topics?
  • Does each topic represent a real customer scenario group?
  • Are our topic names easy to understand?
Prompt checklist
  • Do the prompts sound like natural customer questions?
  • Are the broadest prompts specific enough to be useful?
  • Have we removed obvious overlaps or wording duplicates?
  • Would each prompt help us learn something meaningful?
  • Does each prompt fit clearly within its topic?
Trial checklist
  • Is the prompt bank focused enough for the size of the trial?
  • Are we keeping the core set stable long enough to establish a baseline?
  • Does the mix reflect the questions we most want to test first?
  • Have we prioritized clarity over volume?
  • Can we explain why the current mix is the right one for this trial?
Working principle

A successful trial prompt bank does not need to answer every possible question. It needs to represent the right questions clearly enough to generate useful insight.

Final takeaway: Build the first version with discipline, keep it focused, refine only where needed, and let the trial teach you where expansion will matter most.
