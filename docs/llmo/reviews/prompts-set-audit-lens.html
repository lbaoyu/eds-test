Audit Principles

Start with the right mental model

The most important shift is to evaluate the prompt bank as a measurement system, not as a pile of search queries. A strong prompt set should generate stable, interpretable trend lines, reveal where the brand is visible or absent, and lead to clear optimization actions.

Principle 1

Realistic

The prompts should sound like what real people would ask AI assistants, not keyword fragments disguised as questions.

Principle 2

Specific

Each prompt should contain enough context to narrow the answer space and reduce ambiguity.

Principle 3

Repeatable

Prompts should be structured well enough that changes in results reflect market or content changes, not random prompt noise.

Key audit insight: Weak prompts do not just create poor answers—they create poor measurement. That means the audit should focus not only on whether a prompt is "good writing," but on whether it is a useful instrument for tracking visibility, mentions, citations, and opportunity areas over time.
What a strong library usually does
  • Focuses on a manageable number of priority business areas
  • Includes both branded and unbranded discovery moments
  • Covers multiple stages of the customer journey
  • Uses natural, conversational phrasing
  • Produces insights that can translate into content or optimization work
What a weak library usually does
  • Spreads prompt volume too thinly across too many topics
  • Over-indexes on vanity or branded-only prompts
  • Contains many vague prompts with little context
  • Duplicates similar ideas without adding measurement value
  • Reflects internal site structure more than real user behavior
Level 1

Category-level quality aspects

Categories should define the broad business territories the customer actually wants to measure in AI search. At this level, you are judging strategic structure more than wording.

What to check
  • Strategic relevance: Does the category map to a business priority, product line, service line, or reputation area that matters?
  • Clear scope: Is it obvious what belongs in the category and what does not?
  • Distinctness: Does it avoid overlapping heavily with neighboring categories?
  • Business usefulness: If performance changes in this category, will the customer care?
  • Pilot fit: Is the category set small and focused enough for a 100–200 prompt trial?
  • Market relevance: Does it make sense for the customer's target countries, languages, or business regions?
Common issues
  • Categories mirror a website menu instead of actual demand patterns
  • Multiple categories represent nearly the same idea
  • One category gets most prompts while others are nearly empty
  • The customer tries to cover every line of business in an early pilot
  • Category labels are vague, internal, or ambiguous
  • Important revenue or reputation areas are missing entirely
Category dimension Strong signal Weak signal Typical recommendation
Strategic importance Directly tied to a key business objective Low-impact or generic grouping Remove or de-prioritize low-value categories
Boundary clarity Clear inclusion/exclusion logic Frequent overlap and confusion Rename, merge, or redraw scope boundaries
Portfolio balance Prompt volume aligns to business priority Volume is arbitrarily distributed Reallocate prompts toward top categories
Trial appropriateness Focused enough for depth Too broad for a pilot prompt budget Concentrate on fewer categories first
Practical recommendation: For a small trial, category count should stay intentionally tight. Shallow coverage across many business areas usually looks comprehensive, but it often weakens the value of the insights because no category gets enough prompt depth to reveal clear patterns.
Level 2

Topic-level quality aspects

Topics are where the audit becomes more operational. A topic should represent a specific opportunity area within a category—narrow enough to be meaningful, broad enough to support a useful cluster of prompts.

Assess these qualities
  • Specificity: Is the topic focused on one clear use case, need state, or decision area?
  • User alignment: Does it reflect how users think and ask, rather than how the company organizes itself?
  • Actionability: If visibility is weak here, can the customer identify what content or optimization work to do next?
  • Prompt depth: Does the topic have enough prompt support to produce meaningful data?
  • Coherence: Do the prompts within the topic belong together semantically?
  • Journey-stage clarity: Is the topic mostly early stage, comparison stage, decision stage, or support stage?
Red flags
  • The topic is too broad to optimize meaningfully
  • The topic mixes unrelated user intents
  • The topic duplicates a neighboring topic with slightly different naming
  • The prompts within the topic span too many product lines or personas
  • The topic is too thin to create reliable reporting
  • The topic name is an internal label with no obvious user meaning

Example: broad topic vs refined topic

Weak

Topic: "Banking"

  • Too broad to diagnose clearly
  • Likely to mix education, trust, products, rates, support, and comparison intent
  • Hard to connect weak visibility to a specific optimization action
Stronger

Topic: "Best checking accounts for students"

  • Clear audience and use case
  • Easier to group realistic prompts around one decision area
  • Weak visibility would point toward obvious content gaps or messaging gaps
Best review question at topic level: "If this topic underperforms, can the customer tell what to improve?" If the answer is no, the topic is probably too broad, too mixed, or too poorly defined.
Level 3

Prompt-set quality aspects across the full library

This is the portfolio review. Even if many individual prompts are decent, the overall set can still be weak if it lacks balance, strategic focus, or enough stability to support a clean baseline.

Coverage

Review whether the library covers the customer's priority business areas, high-value decision moments, competitor comparison scenarios, and trust or support use cases.

Balance

Check mix across branded and unbranded prompts, customer journey stages, prompt types, personas, markets, and decision contexts.

Measurement fitness

The set should be stable enough to establish trends. Excessive churn in the prompt bank weakens benchmark quality.

Audit dimensions for the full set
  • Branded vs unbranded mix: Is the customer balancing brand-name prompts with generic discovery intent?
  • Journey-stage coverage: Are awareness, comparison, decision, and support moments all represented?
  • Intent diversity: Does the set include informational, evaluative, comparative, objection-handling, how-to, and purchase-oriented prompts?
  • Competitive coverage: Are substitution and comparison prompts present where the category is competitive?
  • Localization: Are prompts market-native, not merely translated?
  • Duplicate control: Are near-duplicates minimized unless they add genuine measurement value?
Questions to ask in the review
  • Is the prompt bank grounded in real demand, or mostly brainstormed?
  • Is the customer trying to monitor too many topics for the available volume?
  • Would a weak result on this set lead to clear optimization recommendations?
  • Does the set over-index on low-value "what is" prompts?
  • Are later-stage "help me choose" prompts sufficiently represented?
  • Would this prompt bank create a stable baseline for a trial?
Portfolio dimension What good looks like What weak looks like
Strategic focus Concentrated on a smaller number of high-priority areas Thinly spread across too many priorities
Intent mix Balanced across education, comparison, selection, and support Mostly generic informational prompts
Brandedness Brand-name prompts supplemented by unbranded discovery prompts Mostly branded prompts or mostly generic prompts without strategy

Important audit lens: A prompt set can be "large" and still be strategically weak. More prompts do not automatically create better insight. What matters is whether the prompt portfolio is well structured, intentionally balanced, and deep where it matters most.
Level 4

Individual prompt quality aspects

This is the line-by-line review. At this level, the job is to decide whether each prompt is a strong candidate to produce reliable, meaningful, and actionable measurement.

Core prompt checks
  • Realistic: Sounds like a real person asking an AI assistant
  • Single-intent: Focused on one need, one decision, or one task
  • Specific: Provides enough context to narrow the answer space
  • Neutral: Avoids leading language that biases the model
  • Repeatable: Structured well enough to support trend analysis
  • Actionable: If results are weak, the customer can diagnose a content or visibility gap
  • Properly assigned: Fits the right topic and category
Typical prompt-level flaws
  • Too vague to measure reliably
  • Too broad to indicate a specific opportunity
  • Contains multiple questions in one line
  • Feels like a keyword phrase rather than a natural prompt
  • Overly leading or biased toward a desired brand answer
  • Redundant with another prompt without adding a new angle
  • Assigned to the wrong topic or wrong journey stage

Prompt refinement examples

Weak prompt

"Best CRM software"

  • Too broad
  • No audience or business context
  • Likely to create unstable, generic answers
Refined prompt

"What is the best CRM for a B2B SaaS company with more than 5,000 employees?"

  • Clear scenario
  • Narrower comparison set
  • More useful for benchmarking visibility and citations
Weak prompt

"Why is Brand X the best bank?"

  • Leading phrasing
  • Biased toward the desired outcome
  • Poor measurement signal
Refined prompt

"What are the best online banks for college students in the UK?"

  • Natural user language
  • Clear audience and market
  • Useful for brand presence and competitor comparison analysis

Scoring Framework

Use a simple scoring model to make the audit consistent

A lightweight scoring method keeps the review objective and easier to defend. The goal is not to create a perfect mathematical model—it is to create a clear way to sort what should be kept, revised, merged, or removed.

Score Meaning Use when Recommended action
2 Strong The item is strategically sound and ready to keep Retain as is, or only make minor wording edits
1 Needs revision The idea is valid, but execution is weak Rewrite, narrow, reclassify, or rebalance
0 Low value The item is redundant, too vague, or not strategically useful Remove, merge, or replace

Customer Deliverable

How to structure your final feedback

The best customer-facing audit is concise at the top, diagnostic in the middle, and prescriptive at the end. It should show that you reviewed the prompt bank systematically and that your recommendations are connected directly to measurement quality and business value.

1

Executive summary

Overall quality, biggest strengths, biggest gaps, and top recommendations.

2

Taxonomy review

Findings on category and topic structure, overlap, missing areas, and over-breadth.

3

Prompt quality review

Patterns in vagueness, duplication, brandedness, localization, and intent coverage.

4

Recommended revisions

What to keep, merge, split, rewrite, add, and de-prioritize.

5

Trial plan

How to preserve a stable baseline and sequence improvements without breaking trend lines.

Suggested executive-summary language
  • The prompt set has strong strategic intent but needs tighter topic boundaries
  • The library is too broad for the current pilot size and should be concentrated
  • The set includes valuable branded prompts but needs more non-branded discovery prompts
  • Many prompts are directionally correct but require more context to improve repeatability
Recommendation buckets
  • Keep: strong items ready for monitoring
  • Revise: good ideas with weak execution
  • Merge: overlapping categories, topics, or prompt clusters
  • Split: broad topics that need sharper boundaries
  • Add: missing prompt types or opportunity areas
  • De-prioritize: low-value items that dilute pilot quality
Best positioning for recommendations: Frame changes in terms of measurement quality, signal clarity, and actionability. Customers usually respond better when they see that a prompt rewrite is not cosmetic—it improves the reliability of the insights they will get from the product.
Priority Recommendations

High-impact quick wins to look for in almost every audit

If you need to move quickly, these are the changes most likely to improve the quality of the prompt bank before measurement begins or early in the trial.

Quick Win 1

Reduce breadth

If the trial set tries to cover too many business areas, consolidate around a smaller number of top-priority categories and topics.

Quick Win 2

Rewrite vague prompts

Add audience, scenario, geography, or decision context so the prompt behaves more like a repeatable test case.

Quick Win 3

Add comparison intent

Many prompt sets underweight "help me choose" and competitor comparison prompts, even though those are often high-value moments.

Quick Win 4

Fix topic overlap

Merge duplicate topics and split overly broad ones so reporting becomes more interpretable.

Quick Win 5

Balance branded and unbranded

A healthy library should not rely only on brand-name prompts. Generic discovery moments matter just as much.

Quick Win 6

Preserve baseline stability

Keep the majority of the prompt bank stable long enough to establish a benchmark before making aggressive changes.

Bottom line: A strong Adobe LLM Optimizer prompt audit should improve four things at once: structure, coverage, prompt quality, and measurement reliability. When those four improve together, the customer gets cleaner insights and better recommendations.
