Prompt Bank Creation Checklist

A practical checklist to help customers build a strong prompt bank for Adobe LLM Optimizer. Use this guide to create a library that is focused, realistic, measurable, and actionable across categories, topics, and individual prompts.

Phase 1

Before you start

Do not begin by writing random prompts. Start by deciding what business outcomes and visibility questions matter most. A strong prompt bank comes from a clear strategy, not just brainstorming.

1

Choose a focus area

Pick the business area, product line, service line, or reputation area you want to monitor first.

2

Define the markets

List countries, languages, and regions that matter for this prompt bank.

3

Gather demand signals

Use real inputs such as search data, site content, FAQs, sales conversations, support themes, and competitor language.

4

Identify high-value moments

Find where users ask for recommendations, comparisons, how-to help, or decision support.

5

Set pilot boundaries

Decide what not to include yet, so the bank stays focused and measurable.

6

Plan for stability

Keep the initial set stable long enough to establish a baseline before making major changes.

Preparation Checklist
  • I know which business area or product I want to prioritize first
  • I know which countries or languages this prompt bank should cover
  • I have reviewed real customer demand signals, not just internal assumptions
  • I understand the main competitor set or substitute solutions in this space
  • I have identified the user journeys that matter most: awareness, comparison, decision, support, trust
Avoid This
  • Do not try to cover the entire company in the first version of the prompt bank
  • Do not build categories directly from internal org charts or website navigation alone
  • Do not rely only on branded prompts
  • Do not build the whole library from generic "what is" prompts
  • Do not rewrite the full prompt bank too often once measurement has started
Tip: If you only have room for a limited number of prompts, depth beats breadth. It is better to monitor a smaller set of important opportunity areas well than to monitor many areas weakly.
Phase 2

Create categories

Categories are the broad business areas you want to measure in AI search. A category should represent a meaningful area of brand visibility, not just a miscellaneous bucket.

What a strong category does
  • Represents a real business priority or strategic area
  • Has a clear boundary so you know what belongs in it
  • Is different enough from other categories to avoid overlap
  • Would matter if visibility improved or declined in this area
  • Can support multiple topics underneath it
Signs the category needs work
  • The label is too vague to interpret consistently
  • The category overlaps heavily with another category
  • The category exists only because of internal naming, not customer demand
  • It is so broad that almost any prompt could fit into it
  • It is too small or low value to justify tracking in the pilot

Category creation checklist

  • I can explain why each category matters to the business If the reason is unclear, the category is probably not strong enough.
  • Each category has a distinct purpose If two categories feel similar, consider merging or renaming them.
  • The category names are simple, clear, and easy for non-experts to understand
  • The set of categories is focused enough for the size of my pilot

Customer questions to answer

  • Which business areas most need stronger visibility in AI-generated answers?
  • Which categories are likely to generate useful optimization insights?
  • Which categories have enough content and demand to justify monitoring?
  • Which categories can wait until a later phase?
Category test Pass if... Warning if...
Strategic value Pass It clearly supports a real business priority Warning It exists mainly for internal convenience
Boundary clarity Pass You can easily tell what belongs in it Warning It overlaps with neighboring categories
Pilot fit Pass It is focused enough for current prompt volume Risk You are trying to cover too many categories at once
Phase 3

Create topics inside each category

Topics are the specific opportunity areas within a category. Good topics reflect how users explore the space, not just how the website is organized. A topic should be narrow enough to analyze, but large enough to support several prompts.

Specific

Each topic should focus on one clear use case, need state, audience, or decision area.

Actionable

If the topic underperforms, it should be obvious what content or optimization work to consider next.

Coherent

The prompts within a topic should belong together semantically and support the same general opportunity area.

Topic creation checklist

  • The topic reflects a real user need, problem, or comparison moment
  • The topic is more specific than the category above it
  • The topic is not so broad that it mixes unrelated intents
  • The topic is not a duplicate of another topic with different wording
  • The topic can support enough prompts to create meaningful insights
  • The topic makes sense in the target market and language

Topic quality questions

  • Would a weak result on this topic tell me something useful?
  • Can I explain what kind of prompts belong here?
  • Can I explain what does not belong here?
  • Does the topic reflect user behavior rather than internal labeling?
Too broad

Topic: "Credit Cards"

  • Too wide to diagnose clearly
  • Likely to mix student, travel, rewards, low-interest, and application intent
  • Hard to translate into a specific optimization plan
Better topic

Topic: "Best travel credit cards for frequent flyers"

  • Clear use case
  • Clear audience and decision context
  • Easier to group meaningful prompts around one opportunity area
Rule of thumb: If a topic sounds like a giant market rather than a real user question cluster, narrow it further.
Phase 4

Write the prompts

Each prompt should be written as if it were a real user interaction with an AI assistant. A strong prompt is realistic, specific, single-intent, and useful for repeated measurement.

Simple prompt formula

Use this pattern to make prompts more useful and repeatable:

[user goal or question] + [product/service/use case] + [audience or scenario] + [constraint, comparison, or market context]
Prompt writing checklist
  • The prompt sounds like something a real person would ask Use natural language, not keyword fragments.
  • The prompt has one main intent Avoid combining multiple questions in one prompt.
  • The prompt includes enough context to narrow the answer Useful context may include audience, location, use case, skill level, business size, or comparison target.
  • The prompt avoids leading language Do not force the AI toward a desired brand answer.
  • The prompt is specific enough to produce stable measurement over time
  • The prompt fits the correct topic and category
Prompt mix checklist
  • I included both branded and unbranded prompts
  • I included educational prompts for early discovery
  • I included comparison or "help me choose" prompts
  • I included decision-stage prompts related to selection, pricing, fit, or trust
  • I included support or how-to prompts where relevant
  • I included prompts that reflect local market phrasing where needed
Weak prompt patterns
  • "best crm software"
  • "why is brand x the best"
  • "what is banking and what are the best banks and how do I apply"
  • "cheap insurance"
  • Any prompt that is vague, multi-part, or purely promotional
Stronger prompt patterns
  • "What is the best CRM for a B2B SaaS company with over 5,000 employees?"
  • "What are the best travel insurance options for families visiting Japan?"
  • "How does Brand A compare to Brand B for student checking accounts in the UK?"
  • "What is the easiest way to merge PDF files on a Mac?"
  • Prompts with clear user need, scenario, and decision context
Prompt quality test Good prompt Weak prompt
Realism Sounds like a real chat interaction Looks like an SEO keyword list
Intent One main question Several questions bundled together
Specificity Includes audience, use case, market, or constraint Too generic to interpret
Neutrality Open, unbiased framing Leading or brand-biased framing
Actionability Weak results would reveal a clear opportunity Weak results would be too broad to act on
Phase 5

Review, balance, and finalize the prompt bank

Before uploading the prompt bank, run a final review across the whole library. This is where you catch overlap, weak phrasing, missing prompt types, and gaps in market or journey-stage coverage.

Balance

Check whether the library is too narrow, too broad, or too heavily weighted toward one prompt type.

Coverage

Confirm that priority categories, topics, personas, and markets are represented well enough.

De-duplication

Remove near-duplicate prompts unless the phrasing difference adds real measurement value.

Stability

Keep the core set stable enough to create a benchmark before making major changes.

Final quality-control checklist

  • I reviewed all categories for overlap or unclear naming
  • I reviewed all topics for breadth, duplication, and coherence
  • I removed prompts that are too vague or too broad
  • I removed prompts that are duplicates or trivial rewordings
  • I confirmed that every prompt is assigned to the right topic and category
  • I checked that the prompt bank includes high-value comparison and decision prompts
  • I checked that the prompt bank includes relevant support or how-to prompts where needed
  • I checked that the market and language choices are realistic for the audience

Trial-readiness checklist

  • The bank is focused enough to support a clean pilot baseline
  • The majority of prompts can stay stable for the first measurement period
  • I know which prompts are most important to keep stable
  • I know which low-quality prompts I may revise later without disrupting the core baseline
  • I can explain how this prompt bank will generate insights that lead to action
Best final test: Ask, "If visibility is weak on this prompt bank, will I know what to improve?" If the answer is unclear, narrow the categories, sharpen the topics, or rewrite the prompts until the signal becomes more actionable.

Final Self-Assessment

One-page completion checklist

Use this condensed checklist as the final sign-off before the prompt bank is submitted.

Strategy & structure

  • I chose the right focus areas for this pilot
  • My categories are clear and distinct
  • My topics are specific and actionable
  • I did not spread the prompt bank too thinly

Prompt quality

  • My prompts sound like real user questions
  • My prompts are specific and single-intent
  • My prompts avoid biased or overly leading language
  • My prompts fit the right categories and topics

Coverage & balance

  • I included both branded and unbranded prompts
  • I included a healthy mix of informational, comparison, and decision prompts
  • I included support or help prompts where relevant
  • I included market-relevant phrasing and localization

Readiness for measurement

  • The bank is stable enough to establish a baseline
  • I removed weak or duplicate prompts
  • I can explain how this bank will surface visibility gaps and opportunities
  • I am ready to upload and monitor the initial version without major churn
Success criterion: A strong prompt bank should help you answer not just "Is the brand visible?" but also "Where is visibility strong or weak, and what should we do next?"
