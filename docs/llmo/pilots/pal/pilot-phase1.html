Pilot Success Measurement Framework

The pilot should use a fixed before/after measurement method. The same 200 prompts, the same PH market scope, the same competitor set, and the same platform filters should be kept constant across the baseline and post-change measurement windows. This makes the results easier to interpret and supports a clean before/after view of progress.

Recommended Measurement Setup

  • Baseline window: 2–4 weekly snapshots before content changes
  • Post-change window: 4 weekly snapshots after launch of content updates
  • Prompt set: keep the exact same 200 prompts throughout the pilot
  • Scope: PH market, same competitor list, same platform filters
  • Priority URLs: select 8–10 target URLs mapped to the two pilot categories

Why This Works

  • Measures actual movement on the prompts that matter most
  • Supports URL-level and category-level analysis
  • Reduces noise from changing prompt scope mid-pilot
  • Aligns success definition to measurable citation and visibility lift

Metrics Guide with Examples

The section below adds a practical interpretation layer so teams can read the pilot metrics consistently. These examples use simple sample numbers to show what improvement means.

1) Visibility Score

What it means: a roll-up view of how strong the brand's overall presence is in AI-generated answers.

How to read it: if the score moves up after the content changes, the brand is generally becoming more visible and better represented for the tracked prompts.

Example: if Booking & Destinations rises from 42 to 54, that is a meaningful top-line improvement for that category.

2) Unique Prompts with Owned Citations

What it means: how many tracked prompts cited a Philippine Airlines URL at least once.

How to read it: this is the best coverage metric.

Example: baseline = 48 prompts with owned citations; post-change = 63. That means owned citations expanded across 15 additional prompts.

3) Total Times Cited

What it means: total citation volume across the measured prompt set.

How to read it: this tells you whether citation frequency is increasing, even if the number of unique prompts stays similar.

Example: baseline = 120 total citations; post-change = 168. That is a 40% increase in citation volume.

4) Citation Rate

What it means: the share of relevant prompts that cited a specific URL in a given week.

Citation Rate = # prompts that cited the URL in that week ÷ # prompts in the URL's relevant prompt pool

Example: if a baggage policy page has a relevant pool of 20 prompts, and 5 of those prompts cite the page in Week 1, then the Citation Rate is 25%.

5) CRD (Citation Rate Delta)

What it means: the change in citation rate from baseline to post-change average.

CRD = Average post-change citation rate − Baseline citation rate

Example: baseline Citation Rate = 25%; post-change average = 40%. CRD = +15 points.

6) CCS (Citation Consistency Score)

What it means: whether the improvement is durable over time.

CCS = Weeks cited post-change ÷ Total post-change weeks

Example: if a page is cited in 3 out of 4 post-change weeks, CCS = 0.75. That is more durable than a page cited only once in 4 weeks.

7) Prompts Cited In

What it means: how many unique prompts cited a given URL.

How to read it: it indicates breadth at the URL level.

Example: a route page cited in 7 prompts after the change, versus 3 before, is now winning across a broader prompt set.

8) Referral Hits

What it means: user visits coming from AI-generated citations.

How to read it: this is a downstream value signal, not always an early pilot signal.

Example: if a baggage page moves from 12 referral hits to 24, the page is not only being cited more, but is also attracting more human visits from AI experiences.

9) Agentic Hits

What it means: visits from AI agents or crawlers to a page.

How to read it: it helps explain AI demand and retrieval activity, but it is not the same as citation success.

Example: a page can have high Agentic Hits but low citations, which usually means AI systems are checking the page but not selecting it often in answers.

How the Metrics Work Together

  • Visibility Score tells the broad story.
  • Unique Prompts with Owned Citations tells whether coverage improved.
  • Total Times Cited tells whether citation frequency improved.
  • Citation Rate / CRD / CCS tell whether target URLs improved and whether gains lasted.
  • Referral Hits and Agentic Hits add business and retrieval context.

In practice, the strongest pilot story is usually: higher visibility score + more prompts with owned citations + positive CRD across multiple priority URLs + stable or better sentiment.

Suggested Success Thresholds for the Pilot

  • Top-line success: Visibility Score improves for the full pilot prompt set and for at least one of the two focus categories.
  • Citation coverage success: Unique Prompts with Owned Citations improves by 15%+ versus baseline.
  • Citation volume success: Total Times Cited improves by 20%+ versus baseline.
  • URL-level success: At least 5 of 10 priority URLs show positive CRD.
  • Durability success: At least 3 of 10 priority URLs show meaningful post-change consistency, indicated by healthy CCS.
  • Quality guardrail: Sentiment should remain stable or improve.
  • Value signal: Referral Hits from LLMs should trend upward on improved URLs when traffic volume is sufficient to measure.

Minimum Success

Clear improvement in owned citation coverage and positive movement on multiple priority URLs.

Strong Success

Broader citation lift, durable URL-level gains, and visible movement in referral traffic.
