Building a Strong Prompt Bank for Trial Success — Category, Topic, and Prompt Guide

A strong prompt bank is not just a list of questions. It is a structured model for learning where your brand is visible, where customer questions are well served, and where clearer content or positioning may be needed. This guide focuses on the three building blocks that matter most — category, topic, and prompt — and shows how to design them well for a focused, useful trial.

Section 1

What a prompt bank is really doing

A prompt bank is a structured collection of customer-style questions that represents the scenarios, intents, and business priorities you want to monitor during a trial. It helps you move from broad assumptions to a more disciplined view of where your brand is visible, where your content is strong, and where there may be gaps or missed opportunities.

In practice, the prompt bank does three things at once:

Coverage

Defines what you are testing

It determines which business areas, scenarios, and customer questions are included in the trial.

Structure

Organizes the learning

It creates a framework that helps you interpret results by business area and use case instead of looking at disconnected questions.

Quality

Shapes the usefulness of results

If the prompts are too broad, too repetitive, or poorly grouped, the insights will be harder to trust and harder to act on.

Simple way to explain it: A prompt bank is the question framework that determines what a trial can teach you.
Section 2

The core hierarchy: category, topic, and prompt

The most important concept to understand is that a good prompt bank is hierarchical. Categories, topics, and prompts each play a different role. When these levels are confused, the bank becomes harder to structure and harder to interpret.

Level 1

Category

A category is a broad business area or domain. It defines the top-level scope of what you want to learn about.

Examples: Business Banking, Card Services, Loans, Wealth, Digital Banking

Level 2

Topic

A topic is a scenario cluster inside a category. It groups related customer questions into a coherent area of intent.

Examples: Account Opening, Fees and Waivers, Disputes, Cross-border Payments, App Access

Level 3

Prompt

A prompt is an individual customer-style question. It is the actual test case used to observe visibility and performance.

Example: How do I request a credit card annual fee waiver?

Why this hierarchy matters

  • Categories help define strategic scope.
  • Topics help organize customer needs into interpretable clusters.
  • Prompts create the actual question-level signals.

If a category is too narrow, it behaves like a topic. If a topic is too broad, it behaves like a category. If a prompt is too broad, it behaves like a topic. Strong prompt banks keep these levels distinct.

Section 3

How to design strong categories

Categories are the top-level business areas in the bank. They should be stable, strategic, and easy to understand. A category is not a single customer problem. It is the domain under which related problems and questions sit.

A strong category is
  • broad enough to contain several meaningful topics
  • clearly tied to a business priority
  • easy to name and understand
  • stable enough to remain useful over time
A weak category often is
  • too narrow and really just a topic in disguise
  • based on internal jargon rather than a clear business area
  • so broad that it becomes hard to organize meaningfully
  • too low-priority to justify a category on its own
Category design question What "yes" looks like Warning sign
Does this represent a real business area? It aligns to a recognizable domain like Business Banking or Card Services It is a tiny use case or a one-off issue
Can this support several topics? It can hold multiple scenario clusters underneath it It only supports one or two isolated questions
Is the name clear? Most stakeholders would understand it immediately It depends on internal shorthand or ambiguous wording
Is it worth measuring? Performance in this area would matter to the business It is too minor or too incidental to justify the structure
Category example

Weak category

Annual Fee Waiver

This is too narrow. It is better treated as a topic inside a broader category such as Card Services.

Strong category

Card Services

This is broad enough to support multiple topics such as waivers, disputes, transactions, access, and payments.

Section 4

How to design strong topics

Topics are the most important structural layer in many prompt banks. They translate a broad business area into interpretable groups of customer need. A topic should be a coherent scenario cluster — not too broad, not too fragmented, and not just a single prompt dressed up as a theme.

Topic purpose

Group related questions

A topic should bring together prompts that belong to the same customer scenario or need area.

Topic value

Make results interpretable

Topics help you see where performance is strong or weak across a cluster, not just one isolated question.

Topic risk

Avoid false precision

Too many narrow topics create fragmentation and make the structure harder to use in a small trial.

Strong topic Why it works Weak topic version Why it struggles
Fees and Waivers Groups related questions about fees, charges, and waiver actions Annual Fee Waiver Request Form Timing Too narrow for a trial-scale topic
Cross-border Payments Clear scenario cluster with multiple related customer questions International Transfer Cut-off Before Friday Holiday Too specific to stand alone as a topic
App Access and Login Unifies login, verification, token, and access issues under one clear theme Forgot App Password With New Device Useful prompt, but too narrow as a topic

How to tell whether a topic is well designed

  • It should describe a cluster of related customer questions, not just one question.
  • It should be specific enough that you can tell what belongs inside it and what does not.
  • It should support multiple prompts without becoming a miscellaneous bucket.
  • It should help a stakeholder understand what kind of customer need is being evaluated.
Rule of thumb: If a topic can only support one prompt, it may be too narrow. If almost anything could fit into it, it may be too broad.
Section 5

How to design strong prompts

Prompts are the individual questions in the bank. They should sound like real customer questions, be specific enough to create useful signals, and be distinct enough that each prompt earns its place.

The goal is not to create many small wording variations of the same question. The goal is to represent the right mix of customer intents, journey stages, and branded versus unbranded situations clearly enough to learn something useful.

Natural

Use language that sounds like a real customer question, not a keyword list.

Specific

Include enough context to reduce ambiguity.

Focused

Keep each prompt centered on one clear scenario or need.

Actionable

Choose prompts that can lead to practical interpretation and next steps.

Distinct

Avoid prompts that merely repeat the same intent with cosmetic wording changes.

The three dimensions of a strong prompt mix

  • Brand visibility type: branded and unbranded questions
  • Intent coverage: common reasons customers ask the question
  • Journey coverage: where the question sits in the decision process

A strong bank does not only contain good individual prompts. It also contains a good mix of prompts across these dimensions.

Simple prompt formula

Many strong prompts follow a simple structure: customer type or context + need or problem + scenario.

[Who is asking?] + [What are they trying to do?] + [In what context?] Examples: startup + choose business account + Singapore existing cardholder + request fee waiver + annual fee small exporter + compare payment options + overseas suppliers
Branded and unbranded prompts both matter

A strong trial prompt bank usually includes both branded and unbranded prompts. Branded prompts show how the customer performs when the brand is already in the question. Unbranded prompts are often more important for measuring discoverability and competitive visibility.

Prompt type What it helps test Example When it is especially useful
Branded How the brand appears when the user already has the brand in mind How do I request an annual fee waiver for my HSBC credit card? Service, support, product usage, brand-specific decision moments
Unbranded How the brand performs in broader category discovery or comparison What is the best business bank account for startups in Singapore? Awareness, discoverability, comparison, competitor visibility
Trial guidance: There is no single perfect branded versus unbranded ratio for every trial. In a limited prompt bank, it is reasonable to skew the mix intentionally based on the goal — for example, more unbranded prompts for discovery, or more branded prompts for support and product experience. What matters is that the mix is deliberate, not accidental.
Common intent groups to cover

Intent coverage should reflect the most common reasons a customer would ask a question. In practice, these are usually the most useful intent groups to cover.

Intent group What it means Example prompt
Informational / Research The user is learning or understanding the category What should I compare before choosing a business bank account?
Comparative / Evaluation The user is comparing options, brands, or product types Which bank is better for SMEs that make overseas supplier payments?
Planning The user is preparing for a decision or future action What documents do I need before opening a business account in Singapore?
Transactional / Action The user is trying to complete an action or request How do I request an annual fee waiver for my credit card?
Instructional / How-to The user wants step-by-step guidance How do I set up payment approvals online for my business account?
Troubleshooting / Support The user has a problem to solve or a service issue Why was my credit card declined for an overseas online purchase?
Practical prompt-bank rule: Do not overload the bank with only one intent type, such as broad discovery or only support questions. The most useful prompt banks usually include a blend of intent groups.
Customer journey coverage

Good prompt banks do not only cover isolated questions. They cover the customer journey. Even in a limited trial, it helps to represent different stages of how a customer moves from learning to deciding to acting.

Journey stage Typical question pattern Example prompt
Awareness What is this category? What are my options? What is the best business bank account for startups in Singapore?
Consideration How do I compare options? Which features matter? Which bank is better for cross-border payments for small exporters?
Decision What do I need to choose, apply, or proceed? What documents do I need to open a business account in Singapore?
Activation / Getting Started How do I begin using the product or service? How do I set up online approval flows for business payments?
Use / Support How do I resolve issues or complete recurring tasks? How do I report an unauthorised card transaction?
Trial guidance: A limited trial does not need equal volume in every stage. It should, however, avoid overconcentrating only on one part of the journey unless that is the explicit objective of the trial.

Section 6

Common structural mistakes

Most weak prompt banks do not fail because the underlying business questions are wrong. They fail because the structure does not separate category, topic, and prompt clearly enough.

Mistake 1

Using topics as categories

Example: making "Annual Fee Waiver" a category instead of placing it under a broader category like Card Services.

Mistake 2

Using prompts as topics

Example: creating a topic called "Why was my transfer delayed?" that only supports one narrow question.

Mistake 3

Making topics too broad

Example: using "Support" as a catch-all topic that mixes login issues, disputes, waivers, and payments.

Mistake 4

Making prompts too generic

Example: using many broad prompts without enough context to interpret the results well.

Most useful diagnostic question: If you removed the prompt text and only kept the topic names, would the structure still make sense to a business stakeholder? If not, the topic design likely needs work.
Section 7

How to build the bank step by step

Building a prompt bank is not a one-time writing exercise. It is an iterative process: select the right scope, draft the first version, refine what is weak, then hold the bank stable long enough to learn from it.

1

Choose scope

Start with the business area or customer journey that matters most for the trial.

2

Set categories

Choose high-level business domains that justify structured learning.

3

Create topics

Group related customer scenarios into interpretable clusters.

4

Draft prompts

Write realistic questions with enough context to be useful.

5

Balance the mix

Check branded/unbranded mix, intent coverage, and journey coverage.

6

Stabilize

Keep the first version steady long enough to create a meaningful baseline.

Review question What to look for
Are the categories meaningful? They should represent real business areas, not internal shorthand or arbitrary buckets.
Are the topics usable? They should be broad enough to form a cluster, but not so broad that everything fits inside them.
Are the prompts too broad? Look for generic questions without enough customer or scenario context.
Are prompts overlapping? Remove near-duplicates unless each one adds a clearly different intent or scenario.
Is the mix too narrow? Check whether one intent type or one journey stage dominates too much.
Can you explain why each prompt is there? If not, it may not be adding enough value.
Working principle: The goal is not to create the most prompts. The goal is to create the most useful structure and the most useful prompt mix for the trial you are actually running.
Section 8

Trial readiness checklist for category, topic, and prompt quality

Use this checklist before finalizing a prompt bank for a trial.

Category checklist
  • Is each category a real business area?
  • Can each category support several topics?
  • Are the names clear and easy to understand?
  • Would performance in this category matter to the business?
Topic checklist
  • Does each topic represent a real customer scenario cluster?
  • Is the topic broad enough to support several prompts?
  • Is it narrow enough to stay interpretable?
  • Have we avoided too many one-prompt topics?
Prompt checklist
  • Do the prompts sound like natural customer questions?
  • Are the broadest prompts specific enough to be useful?
  • Have we removed obvious overlap or wording variants?
  • Does each prompt fit clearly within its topic?
  • Do we have a deliberate branded and unbranded mix?
  • Do we cover the common intent groups we care about?
  • Do we cover the journey stages that matter most?
Final takeaway: Strong prompt banks are built by separating the roles of categories, topics, and prompts clearly — and by making sure the prompt layer has a deliberate mix across intent, journey stage, and branded versus unbranded questions.
