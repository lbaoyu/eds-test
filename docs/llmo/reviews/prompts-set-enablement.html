Building a Strong Prompt Bank for Trial Success

A prompt bank is one of the most important ingredients in a successful trial. It defines the customer questions you want to test, the business areas you want to learn from, and the visibility opportunities you want to improve. This guide explains how to build a prompt bank that is focused, practical, and ready to generate useful insights.

Section 1

What a prompt bank is

A prompt bank is a structured collection of customer questions that represents the topics and scenarios you want to monitor during a trial. It helps you move from broad assumptions to a more disciplined view of where your brand is visible, where your content is strong, and where there may be gaps or missed opportunities.

Categories

Business areas

These are the major parts of the business you want to explore, such as Business Banking, Card Services, Loans, or Wealth.

Topics

Question clusters

These are the scenario groups inside a category, such as Account Opening, Fees, Rewards, Cross-border Payments, or Disputes.

Prompts

Individual questions

These are the actual customer-style questions that will be used to test visibility and learn where results are strong or weak.

Helpful mental model: Think of the prompt bank as a structured question library. Its job is not just to collect prompts — it is to define what the trial is trying to learn.
Section 2

Why the prompt bank matters

The quality of the prompt bank strongly influences the quality of the trial. A well-built set makes it easier to interpret results, spot meaningful patterns, and decide what to improve next.

A strong prompt bank helps you
  • focus the trial on priority business questions
  • cover a useful mix of customer intents and scenarios
  • spot which areas are performing well and which need attention
  • avoid wasting time on overly broad or low-value questions
  • create a more stable baseline for measuring change over time
A weak prompt bank often leads to
  • overly generic results
  • too much overlap between topics
  • important customer journeys being missed
  • difficulty understanding what to improve
  • noise instead of actionable insight
Key principle: Better prompts produce better insight. A trial does not need the largest possible prompt bank — it needs one that is focused, representative, and practical.
Section 3

How to structure a strong prompt bank

The best prompt banks are built in three layers: business areas, question clusters, and individual prompts. The structure should be simple enough to understand, but detailed enough to reflect real customer needs.

1

Choose the scope

Decide which business area or trial theme matters most right now.

2

Define categories

Use broad business areas that reflect meaningful customer needs.

3

Group into topics

Organize related questions into clear scenario clusters.

4

Write prompts

Create realistic, natural-language questions for each topic.

5

Refine and simplify

Remove overlaps, reduce vague prompts, and keep the structure clear.

Layer Purpose Good practice
Category Defines the business area Use a small number of clear, meaningful categories
Topic Groups related customer scenarios Make each topic focused enough to be interpreted easily
Prompt Tests a customer-style question Use natural phrasing and enough context to be specific
Best practice for trials: It is usually better to go deeper on a smaller number of topics than to spread a limited prompt bank too thinly.
Section 4

What good prompts look like

A good prompt feels like a real customer question. It should be clear, specific, and focused enough to produce useful and interpretable results.

Specific

Include enough context to make the question meaningful and not overly broad.

Natural

Write the prompt the way a real customer might ask it.

Focused

Keep each prompt centered on one clear question or scenario.

Useful

Choose prompts that can lead to practical interpretation and action.

Example: broad vs stronger prompt

Too broad

What is the best business bank account?

This is likely to lead to a very generic answer because it does not specify the type of business, use case, or need.

Stronger

What is the best business bank account for startups in Singapore?

This is easier to interpret because it defines the audience and narrows the scenario.

Example: overlapping vs differentiated prompts

Overlapping

How do I waive my annual fee?
How do I request an annual fee waiver?

These are very close in meaning and may not both be needed.

Better differentiated

How do I request an annual fee waiver?
Why was my annual fee waiver request rejected?

These questions are related, but each serves a more distinct purpose.

Section 6

Trial readiness checklist

Use this checklist before finalizing a prompt bank for a trial.

Structure checklist
  • Have we chosen a clear and focused category or business scope?
  • Are the topics broad enough to form useful clusters, but narrow enough to stay clear?
  • Have we avoided creating too many one-prompt topics?
  • Does each topic represent a real customer scenario group?
Prompt checklist
  • Do the prompts sound like natural customer questions?
  • Are the broadest prompts specific enough to be useful?
  • Have we removed obvious overlaps or wording duplicates?
  • Would each prompt help us learn something meaningful?
Trial checklist
  • Is the prompt bank focused enough for the size of the trial?
  • Are we keeping the core set stable long enough to establish a baseline?
  • Does the mix reflect the questions we most want to test first?
  • Have we prioritized clarity over volume?
Working principle

A successful trial prompt bank does not need to answer every possible question. It needs to represent the right questions clearly enough to generate useful insight.

Final takeaway: Build the first version with discipline, keep it focused, refine only where needed, and let the trial teach you where expansion will matter most.
