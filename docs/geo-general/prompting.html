The prompt is the unit.

How GEO tools turn natural-language questions into brand measurement — where the data comes from, how it's run, and where the numbers stop being trustworthy.

9 min readThe AI-visibility category, mappedVendor-neutral

For twenty years, the atomic unit of search marketing was the keyword. Generative engine optimization keeps the discipline but swaps the unit.

You used to pick the phrases people typed into Google, track where you ranked, and optimise toward position one. GEO — or answer engine optimization, AEO — now tracks the prompt: a full, natural-language question sent to an AI engine like ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Claude, or Google's AI Overviews. Whether your brand gets named in the answer, not whether a blue link ranks, is what these tools measure.

That single substitution changes almost everything about how the tooling works. Here is what's actually happening under the hood across the category — and where the honest limits are.

Foundations

Why the prompt replaced the keyword

A keyword and a prompt look similar but behave differently. The model reads intent and constraints that a keyword strips away — and the two return very different answers.

keywordbest CRM
promptwhat's the best CRM for a Series A startup with a remote sales team?

Two properties of AI queries make the shift necessary rather than cosmetic. First, prompts rarely repeat verbatim — people phrase the same need a hundred ways, and the engines rewrite queries before they search anyway. Second, intent changes the engine's behaviour: a brand-named prompt is retrieved differently from an open-ended one, even on the same topic. If your strategy starts from a keyword export, you're measuring the artifact of a different system.

This is why the category invented a new metric — "prompt volume" — as the AI-era equivalent of search volume, and why prompt selection, not keyword selection, is now the first strategic decision.

Sourcing

Where the prompts come from

Every GEO tool has to answer the same question: which prompts do we track? There are broadly four methods, and most platforms combine several.

auto-generate

From your setup

Describe your brand, competitors, and topics; the tool proposes a starting set you edit. Fast — but only as good as the model's guess at how buyers phrase things.

manual upload

Your own list

Bring prompts from customer research, sales calls, or a hand-built library. Highest control, most effort.

real-query panels

What people truly ask

Vendors license anonymised streams of actual prompts from opt-in consumer panels, then extrapolate frequency and intent. Says "people ask this, this often" rather than guessing.

first-party signals

Your own data

Search Console queries, on-site search, FAQ analysis, and — for tools that run a chatbot — the questions real users ask in those conversations.

The throughline across serious platforms is the same principle: ground prompts in evidence of real demand, not a whiteboard. A beautifully organised set of invented prompts still measures your imagination.

Execution

How the tools actually run prompts

A GEO tool does not ask each prompt once — it can't. AI answers are non-deterministic: ask ChatGPT the same shopping question twice and you can get two different brand lists. A single answer is noise.

So the tools run each prompt repeatedly on a schedule — daily, weekly, or monthly by plan — across multiple engines, and aggregate. Your visibility score is a rate computed over a distribution of answers, not one response. That's why your dashboard rarely matches what you get by typing the prompt into ChatGPT yourself: you're comparing an aggregate against a single sample.

›run × N  →  parse answer  →  did the brand appear?  →  average

Crucially, almost every mainstream tool runs prompts single-shot: one standalone query, fired fresh, no conversation history. That keeps each measurement repeatable and easy to trace — but it's also the category's biggest simplification.

A single AI answer is noise. The signal only shows up when you run the prompt again and again — and average.

Metrics

What they measure once the answer comes back

Running the prompt is step one; parsing the answer is where the metrics come from. The common signals across tools:

visibility rate

How often your brand is mentioned across your tracked prompts, usually 0–100. A frequency, not a ranking.

position

When you appear, how high you're listed. A brand can appear often yet always sit last — so frequency and prominence stay separate.

citation share

Which domains and URLs the engine pulled in as sources, and your share of them. The closest thing to a "click" here.

sentiment

The tone of how the model describes you.

competitor board

Tools auto-discover rivals from the answers and score them on the same math — so you see who's winning the prompts you're losing.

fan-out queries

The searches an engine silently runs before answering. Don't rank on these and you won't be retrieved — where GEO loops back into SEO.

The gap

The single-turn limit nobody fully solves

Real AI use is multi-round. People ask, the model asks back, they refine, they follow up — panel data shows users moving from awareness to consideration to a named brand all inside one conversation. Yet the tools track single, isolated prompts.

That gap is worth being clear-eyed about. Single-shot measurement captures the decisive moment — given a fully-specified intent, does the brand surface? — but it can't model path dependence (does a follow-up flip the recommendation?), memory and personalisation for logged-in users, or the model's own clarifying questions.

The workarounds: compress a conversation's context into one self-contained prompt; model a journey as a sequence of separate prompts; or run a short conversation manually and track the question it lands on. All three approximate; none fully replaces the multi-turn reality. Anyone selling single-shot tracking as the whole picture is overselling the category.

Craft

What separates a good prompt set from a noisy one

Across every vendor's guidance, the same principles recur. Ground prompts in real demand, then cover intent, not keywords — span the buying journey and the personas that matter, with a deliberate mix of branded and unbranded questions rather than a copied ratio.

noisybest project management tool
steadybest PM tool for an 8-person design agency that needs client proofing?

Keep each prompt a single, specific, natural question; because models understand rephrasing, you don't need ten variants of one intent the way SEO did. Put enough prompts under each topic that one stray answer can't swing the trend. Prune prompts that never return a citation. And revisit the set on a cadence, because the landscape shifts monthly. In short: prompts behave like test cases, and a good test case is realistic, specific, and repeatable.

Honesty

Reading the numbers without fooling yourself

The most useful thing any of these tools can do is state its own edges — and the better vendors now do. A few boundaries apply to the whole category, whichever platform you use.

No one can guarantee placement

The engines control ranking and don't expose it. Treat any promise of guaranteed AI citations with suspicion.

Scores move when the engines change

A model update can shift your visibility even if your site didn't. When a step-change hits many brands at once, suspect the engine first.

Answers vary by user, session, and region

The score is an aggregate over repeated samples, not a fixed truth about what everyone sees.

Attribution is not incrementality

Connecting AI-referred traffic to conversions reports association — not proof the purchase wouldn't have happened anyway.

Critics sometimes dismiss simulated-prompt tracking as probabilistic guesswork, and there's a grain of truth: these numbers are a well-instrumented estimate of a moving target, not a meter reading. That's not a reason to ignore them — it's a reason to use them right. Measure often, read the per-engine detail rather than one blended figure, act on the gaps, and judge direction over time.

Outlook

Where it's heading

The direction of travel is clear: prompt datasets grounded in real conversations are becoming the demand layer of the AI era, the way keyword volume was for search. The open frontiers are conversational measurement, personalisation and memory effects, and tighter attribution from "were we mentioned" to "did it drive revenue."

For now, the discipline rests on one idea worth internalising regardless of which tool you buy:

In GEO, the prompt is the unit of truth — and every number you report inherits the quality of the prompts you chose to track.
